At 225 lbs with 6 years of training experience, active Retatrutide therapy, and a structured program, you are in a prime position to simultaneously shed significant body fat and rebuild lean muscle. How the Recomp Protocol Works

  • High Protein Anchor (230g/day): Protein stays constant every single day. This is non-negotiable — it drives muscle protein synthesis, preserves lean mass in a deficit, and keeps you full.
  • Strategic Carb Placement: 140g of carbs timed around training (pre/post workout) to fuel performance and recovery while keeping the deficit intact.
  • Aggressive Caloric Deficit (2,100 cal): Reta manages appetite so you won't feel deprived. This drives fat loss at ~1.2–1.5 lbs/week while keeping energy for training.
  • Progressive Overload Training: The stimulus for muscle growth comes from systematically increasing weight/reps every session. Without this, recomp doesn't work.
  • Retatrutide Synergy: Your active Reta protocol enhances appetite regulation, insulin sensitivity, and visceral fat targeting — making recomp significantly easier.
  • Peptide Support: BPC-157 and TB-500 for your injury recovery, Tesamorelin for visceral fat, CJC/Ipamorelin for sleep and GH — all critical recomp accelerators.

Retatrutide (Reta) — Active Protocol

CURRENTLY ON

You're already on Retatrutide — great. Reta is a triple-hormone receptor agonist (GIP/GLP-1/Glucagon) and the most powerful metabolic compound available for your goals. It handles appetite suppression, visceral fat targeting, insulin sensitivity, and metabolic rate enhancement simultaneously.

  • Titration Reference: Standard titration is 1mg → 2mg → 4mg → 8mg → 12mg, increasing every 4 weeks based on tolerance
  • GI Side Effects: Nausea, constipation, and reduced appetite are common — these typically improve at each dose after 1–2 weeks
  • Injection: Subcutaneous, once weekly, same day each week
  • Hydration: Critical — Reta can cause dehydration. Your 100oz water target + electrolytes are mandatory, not optional
  • Synergy with Training: Reta preserves lean mass during aggressive deficit, making it the perfect complement to our recomp protocol
  • Monitor: Log energy levels, appetite, GI symptoms, and weight weekly. Report any unusual side effects to your coach and Dr. Derrick

Growth Hormone (HGH) — Discuss with Physician

INTERESTED

You've expressed interest in Growth Hormone. At 38 with 30% BF, natural GH production is likely declining. Exogenous GH can dramatically accelerate body composition changes, enhance recovery, improve sleep quality, and support tissue repair — all directly relevant to your goals and injury history.

  • Typical Dose: 2–4 IU/day (subcutaneous, before bed or split AM/PM)
  • Benefits: Fat loss (especially visceral), lean mass preservation, enhanced recovery, better sleep, skin health, joint repair
  • Lab Requirements: IGF-1 levels, fasting glucose, HbA1c monitoring — GH affects insulin sensitivity
  • Alternative: Our CJC/Ipamorelin + Tesamorelin peptide stack provides many of the same benefits by stimulating your own GH production, without the cost and complexity of exogenous GH. Try the peptide stack first and reassess at 12 weeks.
  • Important: Requires physician oversight. Discuss with Dr. Derrick and get baseline IGF-1 levels before starting.

TRT (Testosterone Replacement) — Get Labs First

LABS REQUIRED

At 38 years old with 30% body fat, a new baby disrupting sleep, and moderate stress — your testosterone levels may be suboptimal. High body fat increases aromatase activity (converts testosterone to estrogen), and poor sleep directly suppresses testosterone production. We strongly recommend comprehensive hormone labs.

  • Labs to Request: Total Testosterone, Free Testosterone, SHBG, Estradiol (sensitive assay), LH, FSH, Prolactin
  • When to Test: Fasted, before 9 AM (testosterone peaks in the morning)
  • What We're Looking For: Total T below 400–500 ng/dL, Free T below reference range, elevated estradiol, low SHBG
  • Why It Matters: Optimized testosterone dramatically improves recomp results — more muscle gained, more fat lost, better energy, improved mood, better sleep, higher motivation
  • Previous Compound Experience: You've used Clenbuterol and Anavar before, so you understand PED pharmacology. TRT is a therapeutic, medically supervised approach — discuss with Dr. Derrick based on your lab results.
  • Impact on Program: If labs show low T and you start TRT, we'll likely increase training volume and potentially adjust macros upward. Your body's capacity for muscle growth and recovery increases significantly.

Daily Temperature Check

METABOLIC TRACKING

Why We Track Your Morning Temperature

Your waking body temperature is one of the most underused and powerful biomarkers in coaching. It gives us a direct, daily window into your metabolic rate, thyroid function, recovery status, and overall hormonal health — all without a blood draw. This single data point, tracked consistently, can reveal problems weeks before they show up on lab work or the scale.

How to Take Your Temperature

  • When: Immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed, before drinking water, before any activity. Even if the baby just woke you up — thermometer first, then baby.
  • How: Use a digital oral thermometer. Place under your tongue for the full reading cycle (usually 30–60 seconds). Do not talk or breathe through your mouth.
  • Where to log: Record it in your daily check-in in the FBF app under the body/notes section
  • Keep your thermometer on your nightstand — if you forget and get up first, the reading is no longer valid for that day
  • Consistency is key: Same thermometer, same method, every single morning. The trend over days and weeks is what matters, not any single reading.

What Your Temperature Tells Us

Metabolic Rate & Thyroid Function

Your basal body temperature is a direct reflection of your metabolic rate — how much energy your body is producing at rest. Thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) are the primary regulators.

  • Healthy range: 97.8°F – 98.6°F upon waking indicates a well-functioning metabolism and adequate thyroid output
  • Consistently below 97.6°F is a red flag for metabolic downregulation — your body is slowing its engine. This is the earliest warning sign that calories may be too low or thyroid function is suppressed.
  • If we see a sustained drop, we can increase calories (refeed), adjust carb timing, or flag it for Dr. Derrick to check thyroid labs — before you hit a plateau.

Adaptive Thermogenesis & Deficit Response

With an aggressive ~600–700 cal deficit, your body may fight back through adaptive thermogenesis — literally turning down the thermostat to burn fewer calories.

  • A gradual temperature decline (0.3–0.5°F over 2–3 weeks) tells us your metabolism is adapting. Normal to a degree, but if it drops too far, we act.
  • Strategic refeeds: Temporary calorie increase (especially carbs) to reset leptin signaling and bump thermogenesis back up.
  • Reta interaction: Retatrutide can affect thermoregulation. Tracking temperature helps us distinguish between normal Reta effects and genuine metabolic suppression.

Recovery, Stress & Overtraining

With a new baby, disrupted sleep, work stress, and 5 days of training — recovery monitoring is critical for you.

  • A sudden temperature drop (0.5°F+ from your baseline) after a hard training week can indicate overreaching — your nervous system is fatigued
  • A spike above your normal range can indicate acute stress, inflammation, infection onset, or an immune response
  • Cortisol connection: Poor sleep (hello, baby) elevates cortisol, which suppresses thyroid conversion (T4→T3) and lowers waking temperature. If we see this pattern, we address sleep and recovery first.

Hormonal Health & Peptide Response

  • Tracking response to peptides: When we introduce CJC/Ipamorelin and Tesamorelin, we expect improved sleep and a stable or slightly elevated waking temperature over 4–6 weeks.
  • Testosterone correlation: Low waking temperature + fatigue + stalled strength gains can indicate low testosterone — we'd flag for bloodwork.
  • Sleep quality feedback loop: CJC/Ipamorelin enhances deep sleep → better hormone output → higher waking temperature. If temperature isn't trending up after starting peptides, we investigate sleep quality first.

Real Talk: Training with a New Baby

You have a new baby. Sleep will be inconsistent. Energy will fluctuate. Some days will be a grind. This program is designed for your reality — not a fantasy schedule. Here's how we make it work.

Priority Rules (When Life Gets Hard)

  • Priority #1: Sleep. If the baby had you up all night and you got less than 5 hours, SKIP the gym and sleep. One missed workout means nothing. Chronic under-recovery will wreck your progress, your hormones, and your mood.
  • Priority #2: Protein. Hit 230g protein no matter what. Even on the worst day, you can eat chicken and drink a shake. Everything else (carb timing, meal count) is secondary.
  • Priority #3: Steps. 8,000 steps is achievable even with a baby — push the stroller, walk during breaks, pace during calls. Non-negotiable daily NEAT.
  • Priority #4: Training. 5 days/week is the target, but 4 is fine. 3 is the absolute minimum. If you can only do 3, hit Chest/Tris, Legs, and Back/Bis. Cut Shoulders and Cardio/Core into the other days when you can.
  • Priority #5: Everything else. Perfect macro splits, supplement timing, peptide scheduling — these matter, but not more than sleep and protein.

Practical New Parent Tips

  • Train early (5–6 AM): Get it done before the day starts. The baby is usually still asleep, and you won't have to choose between gym and family time later.
  • Meal prep is survival: Sunday prep is mandatory, not optional. When the baby is screaming at 6 PM, you need grab-and-go meals — not a reason to order pizza.
  • Keep protein shakes at your desk: Meal 4 requires zero prep. When work + baby = chaos, this is your insurance policy.
  • Sleep when you can: If the baby naps and you're exhausted, nap. 20–30 minutes can rescue a day. Sleep is anabolic — it's when your body builds muscle and burns fat.
  • Stroller walks count: Weekend 10K+ steps with the family. Push the stroller around the neighborhood, go to the park. Active recovery that's also quality time.
  • Don't compare to pre-baby you: Your lifts may dip in the first 4 weeks while your body adjusts to less sleep. This is normal. They'll come back. Focus on showing up consistently.
  • Communicate with Lindsey: Let your partner know your training schedule. If she knows you're up at 5 AM to train, you can coordinate overnight baby duties. Teamwork.
  • Bad days happen: You'll miss a meal. You'll skip a workout. You'll have a rough night. Log it in the check-in, don't spiral, and reset the next day. Consistency over 12 weeks matters more than any single day.

Caffeine Check

You're currently using pre-workout + coffee + 1 energy drink daily. That's potentially 500–700mg of caffeine. With disrupted sleep and a new baby, you might be tempted to increase caffeine. Don't. Cap total daily caffeine at 400mg. Try to drop the energy drink — replace with LMNT electrolytes for afternoon energy. Excess caffeine disrupts whatever sleep you ARE getting, creating a vicious cycle.
