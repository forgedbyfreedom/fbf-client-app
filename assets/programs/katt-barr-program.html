Important Peptide Notes

Active Compounds

Retatrutide + Tesofensine — Active Protocol

You are already active on both compounds. This section covers maintenance protocols, what to monitor, and when to consider adjustments. No "discuss with your doctor" caveats — you know what you're doing.

Retatrutide (Reta)

Triple-hormone receptor agonist (GIP/GLP-1/Glucagon). You are currently active on Reta — this is your primary metabolic compound.

  • Maintenance Protocol: If you've completed titration and are at a stable maintenance dose, continue at that dose. Reta's effects compound over time — consistency is more important than dose escalation.
  • Appetite Management: At 1,500 cal with Reta, appetite should be well-controlled. If you're struggling to eat enough protein, prioritize protein shakes and casein pudding to hit 145g.
  • Dose Adjustment Triggers: Consider dose adjustment if: (1) appetite suppression is too strong and you can't hit protein targets, (2) GI side effects persist beyond the first 2–3 weeks of a dose increase, (3) weight loss stalls for 3+ weeks despite adherence.
  • GI Management: Eat slowly, chew thoroughly, avoid high-fat meals in a single sitting. Ginger tea and peppermint can help with nausea if it occurs.
  • Metabolic Benefits You Should Be Seeing: Reduced appetite, improved insulin sensitivity, visceral fat reduction, better lipid profile, stabilized blood sugar, reduced systemic inflammation.
  • Titration Reference (if needed): Standard titration is 1mg → 2mg → 4mg → 8mg → 12mg, with each dose held for 4 weeks minimum before escalation. Most women stabilize at 4–8mg maintenance.

Tesofensine (Teso)

Triple monoamine reuptake inhibitor (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine). You are currently active on Tesofensine.

  • Benefits You Should Be Seeing: Enhanced thermogenesis (you may notice you run warmer), improved mood and focus, reduced appetite, increased energy expenditure at rest.
  • Monitoring — Heart Rate & Blood Pressure: Tesofensine can increase resting HR by 5–10 bpm and modestly raise BP. Check resting HR and BP weekly. If resting HR consistently exceeds 90 bpm or BP exceeds 140/90, consider dose reduction.
  • Sleep Considerations: Due to norepinephrine/dopamine activity, take Tesofensine in the morning only. If sleep quality declines, this is the first compound to evaluate timing on.
  • Synergy with Reta: Reta handles appetite and metabolic signaling. Tesofensine adds thermogenesis and mood/focus support. Together, they are one of the most effective recomp compound stacks available.
  • Standard Dosing: 0.25–0.5mg/day for women. Stay at the lowest effective dose — more is not better with Teso.
  • Cycling Consideration: Some practitioners recommend cycling Teso (8–12 weeks on, 4 weeks off) to prevent receptor desensitization. Discuss with your coach based on your response.

Female Hormone Optimization

IMPORTANT

Recommended Hormone Panel

As an experienced female lifter on active compounds and peptides, monitoring your hormonal landscape is critical. Request these labs at baseline and every 12 weeks:

  • Estradiol (E2): Key estrogen marker. Affects body composition, mood, bone density, skin quality, and libido. Optimal range depends on cycle phase — track accordingly.
  • Progesterone: Balances estrogen, affects sleep quality, anxiety, and body composition. Low progesterone is extremely common and often overlooked.
  • DHEA-S: Precursor to both estrogen and testosterone. Affects energy, libido, muscle building capacity, and skin quality. Supplemental DHEA (10–25mg/day) may be appropriate if levels are low.
  • Thyroid Panel (TSH, Free T3, Free T4): Thyroid drives metabolic rate. Caloric deficit and hard training can suppress thyroid output. Free T3 is the active hormone — if it drops below optimal, recomp stalls regardless of adherence.
  • Ferritin & Iron: Women frequently have low ferritin (< 50 ng/mL) due to menstruation. Low ferritin causes fatigue, poor recovery, hair loss, and cold intolerance — often misdiagnosed as thyroid issues. Critical to check.
  • B12: Energy, neurological function, red blood cell production. Deficiency is common and causes fatigue that mimics other issues.
  • Total Testosterone & Free Testosterone: Even in women, testosterone matters for muscle building, energy, libido, and mood. Low levels can impair recomp results.
  • SHBG: Sex hormone binding globulin — affects how much free testosterone and estradiol are available. High SHBG can bind up your hormones and reduce their effectiveness.
  • Cortisol (AM): Stress hormone. Chronically elevated cortisol impairs fat loss, disrupts sleep, and causes water retention. One of the most overlooked barriers to body composition change.

Menstrual Cycle Considerations

  • Follicular Phase (Days 1–14): Estrogen rises, insulin sensitivity is better, energy is higher. Best phase for pushing training intensity and hitting PRs. Carb tolerance is higher.
  • Luteal Phase (Days 15–28): Progesterone rises, body temp increases, water retention is common, cravings may increase. Scale weight may be 2–5 lbs higher — this is water, not fat. Training may feel harder. This is normal.
  • Note on Reta: GLP-1 compounds can affect menstrual regularity, especially during titration. Track your cycle and note any changes — report to your coach.
  • Training Periodization: Push intensity in the follicular phase. In the luteal phase, if energy dips, maintain weights but reduce volume slightly. Do not panic about the scale during luteal phase.
  • Temperature Tracking: Your daily temperature check will naturally be 0.3–0.5°F higher during the luteal phase due to progesterone. This is normal and expected — factor it into the reference table below.

Baseline Assessments

ACTION REQUIRED

Before we begin, we need two baseline data points to track your progress accurately and make informed adjustments throughout your program. Both should be completed within your first week.

1. Blood Lab Panel

Upload to App

Obtain a current or recent comprehensive blood panel (within the last 60 days). If you don't have one, request the following from your physician:

  • Metabolic Panel: Fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HbA1c
  • Lipid Panel: Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides, VLDL
  • Liver Panel: ALT, AST, GGT, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin
  • Kidney Function: BUN, creatinine, eGFR, uric acid
  • Thyroid: TSH, Free T3, Free T4
  • Female Hormones: Estradiol, progesterone, DHEA-S, total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG
  • Inflammation: hs-CRP, ESR, homocysteine
  • CBC: Complete blood count with differential
  • Vitamins/Minerals: Vitamin D (25-OH), B12, ferritin, iron, folate
  • Cortisol: AM cortisol (drawn before 9 AM)

Why This Matters

Your blood work gives us a complete picture of your metabolic and hormonal health before we start. We'll re-test at 12 weeks and 24 weeks to measure improvements in hormones, lipids, liver enzymes, inflammation markers, and micronutrient status. Combined with your body composition changes, these objective markers tell the full story. Upload your results directly in the FBF app under your profile, or send a photo/PDF to your coach via chat.

2. InBody Composition Scan

FREE AT YOUR GYM

Most gyms have an InBody scanner available to members at no charge. Complete your baseline scan within the first week.

  • What it measures: Body fat %, lean muscle mass (per limb), visceral fat level, total body water, basal metabolic rate
  • When to scan: Morning, fasted (same conditions as your weigh-in). Before your Monday workout is ideal.
  • How often: Baseline (Week 1), then every 4 weeks. The InBody tracks skeletal muscle vs. fat changes — this is how we measure recomp, not just the scale.
  • What to expect: Takes ~60 seconds. Stand on the machine, grip the handles, hold still. Ask the front desk to print your results sheet.
  • Upload: Take a photo of your InBody results sheet and upload it to the FBF app with your first check-in, or send it via chat.

Why InBody Over Scale Weight

During a lean recomp at 135 lbs, the scale can be extremely misleading — especially for women with hormonal water fluctuations. You might lose 5 lbs of fat and gain 3 lbs of muscle — the scale only shows a 2 lb change, but your body looks completely different. The InBody scan separates fat from muscle so we can verify the protocol is working. This is the gold standard for tracking recomp.

Daily Temperature Check

METABOLIC TRACKING

Why We Track Your Morning Temperature

Your waking body temperature is one of the most underused and powerful biomarkers in coaching. It gives us a direct, daily window into your metabolic rate, thyroid function, recovery status, and overall hormonal health — all without a blood draw. This single data point, tracked consistently, can reveal problems weeks before they show up on lab work or the scale.

How to Take Your Temperature

  • When: Immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed, before drinking water, before any activity
  • How: Use a digital oral thermometer. Place under your tongue for the full reading cycle (usually 30–60 seconds). Do not talk or breathe through your mouth.
  • Where to log: Record it in your daily check-in in the FBF app under the body/notes section
  • Keep your thermometer on your nightstand — if you forget and get up first, the reading is no longer valid for that day
  • Consistency is key: Same thermometer, same method, every single morning. The trend over days and weeks is what matters, not any single reading.
  • Cycle Note: Your temperature will naturally be 0.3–0.5°F higher during the luteal phase (post-ovulation). This is progesterone-driven and completely normal. Factor this into your trend analysis.

What Your Temperature Tells Us

Metabolic Rate & Thyroid Function

Your basal body temperature is a direct reflection of your metabolic rate. Thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) are the primary regulators. They control how every cell in your body converts nutrients into heat and energy.

  • Healthy range: 97.8°F – 98.6°F upon waking indicates a well-functioning metabolism and adequate thyroid output
  • Consistently below 97.6°F is a red flag for metabolic downregulation — your body is slowing its engine. This is the earliest warning sign that calories may be too low or thyroid function is suppressed.
  • Female-specific: Women are at higher risk for thyroid dysfunction. Low waking temp combined with fatigue, cold hands/feet, hair loss, and irregular cycles warrants immediate thyroid labs.
  • If we see a sustained drop, we can increase calories (refeed), adjust carb timing, or flag it for labs — all before you hit a plateau.

Adaptive Thermogenesis & Diet Response

When you diet, your body can fight back through adaptive thermogenesis — literally turning down the thermostat to burn fewer calories. This is the #1 reason diets stall.

  • A gradual temperature decline (0.3–0.5°F over 2–3 weeks) while in a deficit tells us your metabolism is adapting. Some adaptation is normal, but too much requires intervention.
  • What we do about it: Strategic refeed days (temporary calorie increase, especially carbs) to reset leptin signaling and bump thermogenesis back up.
  • Tesofensine interaction: Teso increases thermogenesis — your waking temperature may actually be slightly higher than baseline. If it drops despite being on Teso, that's a stronger signal that metabolic suppression is occurring.
  • Reta interaction: Reta can affect thermoregulation. Tracking temperature helps us distinguish between normal compound effects and genuine metabolic suppression.

Recovery, Stress & Overtraining

Your temperature reflects your autonomic nervous system balance — the interplay between your sympathetic and parasympathetic systems.

  • A sudden temperature drop (0.5°F+ from your baseline) after a hard training week can indicate overreaching
  • A spike above your normal range can indicate acute stress, inflammation, infection onset, or an immune response
  • Pattern recognition: Over 4–6 weeks, we'll know your personal baseline. Then deviations become incredibly informative.
  • Cortisol connection: Chronically elevated cortisol suppresses thyroid conversion (T4→T3) and lowers waking temperature. If we see this pattern, we address stress and recovery first.

Hormonal Health & Peptide Response

Temperature is a downstream marker of your entire hormonal cascade.

  • Tracking response to peptides: When you add CJC/Ipamorelin and Tesamorelin, we expect improved sleep, better recovery, and a stable or slightly elevated waking temperature over 4–6 weeks.
  • Menstrual cycle tracking: Your temperature naturally shifts across your cycle. Combining daily temp with cycle day tracking gives us a powerful hormonal health dataset.
  • Progesterone marker: A clear temperature shift at ovulation (~0.3–0.5°F rise) confirms healthy ovulation. If this shift disappears, it may indicate anovulatory cycles worth investigating.
  • Sleep quality feedback loop: CJC/Ipamorelin enhances deep sleep → better hormone output → higher waking temperature. If temperature isn't trending up after starting peptides, we investigate sleep quality first.
