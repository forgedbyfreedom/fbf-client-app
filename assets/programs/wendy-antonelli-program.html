Advanced Training Notes

  • Drop Sets: On the last set of indicated exercises, reduce weight by ~30% immediately after failure and rep to failure again. One drop per set.
  • Rest-Pause: Hit failure, rack the weight, rest 10-15 seconds, then rep to failure again. Repeat once more (3 total mini-sets within one set).
  • Myo-Reps: Hit failure on the activation set, rest 5 deep breaths (10-15s), then do 3-5 reps. Repeat 3-4 times. Stop when you can no longer hit 3 reps.
  • Giant Sets: Perform all exercises back-to-back with no rest between. Rest only after completing the full giant set circuit.
  • Pre-Exhaust: Isolation movement before compound to pre-fatigue the target muscle, ensuring it fails before synergists.
  • Deload Week: Every 4th week, reduce volume by 40% and intensity by 20%. Maintain frequency. This is mandatory for recovery at your training intensity.

Cardio Protocol

Cardio Schedule

  • Daily: 12,000 steps minimum — 45-minute walk (morning or evening), plus accumulated steps throughout the day
  • Stairmaster (3x/week — Tue, Thu, Sat): 20 minutes moderate intensity (level 6-8). Post-workout on training days, standalone on Saturday.

Cardio Strategy for Lean Gain

Cardio volume is moderate — enough to support cardiovascular health, maintain insulin sensitivity, and keep body fat in check during the surplus, but not so much that it interferes with recovery or muscle growth. Your 12,000 daily steps are the foundation. The stairmaster and incline walks provide structured cardiovascular conditioning without excessive caloric expenditure. If weight gain exceeds target pace (more than 0.5 lb/week), we increase cardio before reducing calories.

Daily Temperature Check

METABOLIC TRACKING

Why We Track Your Morning Temperature

Your waking body temperature is one of the most underused and powerful biomarkers in coaching. It gives us a direct, daily window into your metabolic rate, thyroid function, recovery status, and overall hormonal health — all without a blood draw. This is especially important during the menopausal transition when hormone fluctuations can impact every system.

How to Take Your Temperature

  • When: Immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed, before drinking water, before any activity
  • How: Use a digital oral thermometer. Place under your tongue for the full reading cycle (usually 30-60 seconds). Do not talk or breathe through your mouth.
  • Where to log: Record it in your daily check-in in the FBF app under the body/notes section
  • Keep your thermometer on your nightstand — if you forget and get up first, the reading is no longer valid for that day
  • Consistency is key: Same thermometer, same method, every single morning. The trend over days and weeks is what matters, not any single reading.

What Your Temperature Tells Us

Metabolic Rate & Thyroid Function

Your basal body temperature reflects your metabolic rate — how much energy your body produces at rest. Thyroid hormones (T3/T4) regulate this process. During menopause, thyroid issues become more common.

  • Healthy range: 97.8°F - 98.6°F upon waking indicates a well-functioning metabolism and adequate thyroid output
  • Consistently below 97.6°F is a red flag for metabolic downregulation — your body is slowing its engine. At 50, this could indicate thyroid suppression (common in menopause) or over-training.
  • Why this matters for you: Your ashwagandha and training support thyroid function, but the menopausal transition can affect thyroid output. Temperature tracking catches declines weeks before lab work would.

Recovery, Stress & Overtraining

With 5 days of intense advanced training techniques plus daily cardio, monitoring recovery is critical.

  • A sudden temperature drop (0.5°F+ from baseline) after a hard training week indicates overreaching — your nervous system is fatigued
  • A spike above normal range can indicate acute stress, inflammation, infection, or immune response
  • Pattern recognition: Over 4-6 weeks, we establish your personal baseline. Deviations tell us when you need a deload week, more sleep, or protocol adjustment
  • Cortisol connection: Chronically elevated cortisol suppresses thyroid conversion (T4 to T3) and lowers waking temperature. Your ashwagandha, infrared sauna, and cold plunge all help manage cortisol.

Hormonal Health & Peptide Response

Temperature is a downstream marker of your entire hormonal cascade — thyroid, testosterone, growth hormone, estrogen, and more.

  • Menopausal fluctuations: Hot flashes cause temperature spikes; estrogen decline can lower baseline temperature. Tracking helps us distinguish between menopause effects and genuine metabolic issues.
  • Peptide response: CJC/Ipamorelin should improve sleep and recovery — expect a stable or slightly elevated waking temp over 4-6 weeks. If temperature doesn't respond, peptide dose or timing may need adjustment.
  • TRT/GH response: Optimized testosterone and GH typically correspond with a higher, more stable waking temperature. This confirms your compounds are working.
